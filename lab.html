Objectives of Lab Reports

This page outlines a general format for lab reports, which you can customize as needed. Lab reports are a common type of document in engineering and can significantly impact your course grade, yet often, little time is spent on mastering how to write them effectively. Each professor may have specific preferences, but the core purpose of lab reports remains consistent: to document your findings and convey their significance. Understanding the format and key components of a lab report will help you adapt to the requirements of your course or professor.

A well-crafted lab report goes beyond simply presenting data; it demonstrates a deep understanding of the underlying concepts. It is not enough to merely list expected and observed results. You must also analyze how and why any discrepancies occurred, explain their impact on the experiment, and show your grasp of the principles being tested. Remember, while a structured format is useful, it cannot substitute for clear thinking and organized writing. Your report should be well-organized and articulate, reflecting a coherent presentation of your ideas.

Selecting A Thesis Lab

Selecting a thesis lab is a critical decision in graduate school that requires careful consideration. Start by reviewing the literature and research outputs of the labs you are interested in to understand their projects and focus. Assess the faculty members' profiles, including their research interests and mentoring styles. Evaluate the lab environment by visiting the lab and observing the working conditions and team dynamics. Additionally, seek feedback from current and former lab members to gain insights into their experiences. Finally, reflect on your own needs and interests to ensure the lab aligns with your personal and professional goals. This thorough approach will help you choose a lab that supports your academic and career development effectively.

A Excellent Lab Mentor

It has been our experience that students who use the advisor’s scientific reputation as the sole criterion for choosing their lab are often discouraged and disappointed. Finding the right fit for you – the person that you can relate to, learn from, and who will put your interests first – should be your goal. As you meet with faculty, asking and hearing the answers to your questions that reflect your interests can be an effective way of identifying this fit. You can only identify an advisor who meets these requirements by interacting frequently and openly with prospective advisors before, during, and after your rotation.

Lab Science Is Challenging

You are going to be spending a lot of time doing it for the next four years, so you may as well enjoy it. But even more importantly, we are best at what we love to do. Science is hard – there is no answer key, and the questions aren’t even provided. To succeed, you have to put all of your focus and energy into the problem that you want to solve. You won’t do this if you don’t love your work, so pick an area that fascinates you. It is important that you are comfortable with the people in your group. Again, research is hard and you don’t want to compound that difficulty by entering a situation that is personally or emotionally challenging. A research environment in which you are treated with respect and are able to bounce ideas off others is what makes for great science. Ideally, you should find an environment and set of colleagues that you will learn from and enjoy professionally.
